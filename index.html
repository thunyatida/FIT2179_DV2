Australia's Vanishing Wild

Australia is home to some of the most unique wildlife on Earth and one of the worst extinction records in the world. This visualisation explores the scale, geography, and drivers of Australia's threatened species crisis, using official data from the Australian Government's EPBC Act and the National Environmental Science Program.

The Scale of the Crisis

Under the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation (EPBC) Act 1999, the Australian Government officially lists species and ecological communities at risk of extinction. As of February 2026, the list contains 2,208 entries and grows every year. Plants account for the largest share, but Australia's mammals and birds have captured global attention for their alarming decline.

"Since European settlement, Australia has lost more mammal species than any other country on the planet and the losses continue."

Where Are They?

Threatened species are not evenly distributed across Australia. New South Wales hosts the most threatened species of any state, driven by high biodiversity and decades of land clearing. Western Australia follows closely, with an extraordinary number of endemic plants found nowhere else on Earth.

Is It Getting Worse?

Every year, new species are added to the threatened list and almost none are removed through recovery. The cumulative total has been rising steadily since 2000, and the annual rate of new listings has increased sharply since 2015. The 2019–20 Black Summer bushfires pushed dozens of already-stressed species closer to extinction.

Cumulative Threatened Species Listed Since 2000

Total EPBC-listed threatened species accumulating over time. Source: DCCEEW.

New Species Added to the List Each Year

Annual new additions to the EPBC threatened list. A growing bar means the crisis is worsening. Source: DCCEEW.

The number of new listings per year has nearly tripled since 2000. The spike visible after 2019 reflects reassessments triggered by the Black Summer fires, which burned over 18.6 million hectares the habitat of more than 800 threatened species.

What Is Being Done?

The Australian Government's National Environmental Science Program (NESP) Threatened Species Recovery Hub funded 141 research projects between 2015 and 2021, spanning the entire continent. Projects ranged from emergency triage of at-risk species to large-scale island conservation programs.

06

What Is Driving Extinction?

No single cause is responsible. Australia's extinction crisis is driven by habitat loss, invasive species, and altered fire regimes all accelerated by climate change. Introduced feral cats kill an estimated 1.5 billion native animals every year.

07

Gone Forever

These are not distant historical losses. Many extinctions have happened within living memory and some within the last decade. Each loss is permanent.

1936

Thylacine (Tasmanian Tiger)

The last known individual died in Hobart Zoo. The species was hunted to extinction under a government bounty scheme.

1960s

Desert Bandicoot · Lesser Bilby

Both central Australian species vanished as predator pressure and habitat degradation intensified after European settlement.

2009

Christmas Island Pipistrelle

A small bat species. Emergency captive breeding was proposed but no action was taken in time. The last individual was recorded in August 2009.

2016

Bramble Cay Melomys

Australia's first mammal extinction directly attributed to climate change. Rising sea levels inundated its only known habitat in the Torres Strait.

2021

Smooth Handfish · 12 other species

Thirteen species officially declared extinct following a post-Black Summer bushfire review by the EPBC Threatened Species Scientific Committee.
